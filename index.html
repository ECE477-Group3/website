Pianos and other keyed instruments are some of the most ubiquitous musical instruments to be found, being utilized in classical and contemporary compositions. While the sounds produced by keyboards extends far beyond the original hammered string tone produced by early pianos thanks to the use of electronic synthesis, the way in which one plays it has changed little since its inception. Notes and chords are all formed utilizing a repetition of keys arranged from lowest to highest note.

Given the widespread use of the piano and other keyed instruments, it can be concluded that this layout works for many established musicians. The primary complaints to this established form stem from those who fall outside of this category. Beginners, casual music makers, and those playing alternative forms of music often complain in regards to the dexterity required to play this instrument. This sets them behind those who are trained to specifically play such keyed instruments and ul.cntrtimately prevents them from being able to perform pieces of music that they strongly desire to play.

In order to remedy the problem described above, our group aims to bring the musical keyboard into the 21st century by transforming it into something that is more natural whilst leaving enough complexity such that one is not limited in their musical explorations by its design. This new interface will borrow heavily from the rise and spread of computing technology, something that is even more ubiquitous than pianos themselves.
